I&#8217;ve been attempting these <a href="https://plasticmind.com/retrospection/30-day-change/">30 day challenges</a> for over half a year now, with considerable success.  August will probably be the most difficult yet rewarding challenge yet: **no television for 30 days**.



			<!--more-->

I&#8217;ve found that with any of the challenges I need to be able to answer the question: &#8220;Why am I attempting this?&#8221;  Last month&#8217;s challenge was to draw something new every day.  I gave up the challenge almost immediately because I couldn&#8217;t answer that question.  Why in the world would I want to draw something new every day?





I&#8217;m a bit ashamed to admit it, but television is a big part of my day.  My mind is typically racing, even when engaged in leisurely activities; so I often find great relaxation by being a told a story.  But all rationalizing aside, I&#8217;m an addict.  Before I married, I used to spend an entire Saturday at the theater, hopping from screen to screen after a film finished.  Nowadays, I satisfy my addiction by immersing myself in a television series (current obsession: <a href="https://doctorwho.bbcamerica.com/">the new Doctor Who</a>).





So I have three answers to the question: **&#8220;Why do I want to give up television for the month?&#8221;**



<ol>
<li>

The first is obvious: watching television takes up a lot of my time.  I&#8217;ll watch television when I&#8217;m eating lunch at work, sometimes on the train ride home, often during dinner and almost always in the evenings with Jessica&#8212;probably three hours in the course of an average day.  That is a lot of time&#8212;time that could be spent doing better things.  I could be investing more in my family, teaching my son the value of being creative, planning better for our family&#8217;s future or our church&#8217;s future.  I want to redeem that time I&#8217;ve been squandering.

</li>
<li>

I don&#8217;t like what my television habit is doing to my children.  My son is already in the habit of asking for a &#8220;moobie&#8221; first thing in the morning, and I can&#8217;t help but think that the hours mom and dad spend in front of the television feed that impulse.

</li>
<li>

Finally, I&#8217;ve been convicted about living more in the realm of cognitive thought than feeling.  It may sound like an obscure reason, but I&#8217;ve been challenged by Tim Challies&#8217; book *<a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0310329035/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=plasticmind-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=217145&amp;creative=399373&amp;creativeASIN=0310329035">The Next Story</a>*.  In one of the early chapters of the book, he talks about how the human brain process images and words differently.  &#8220;An image is processed in an instant, while words take time and sequence.&#8221;  He goes on to reason that &#8220;learning through images and visual media is directly opposed to learning by reading, which requires a more sustained focus and actually generates new skills and capacities in the brain.&#8221;  This month, I plan to test his theory.

</li>
</ol>



Two simple ground rules I&#8217;m setting for myself in this challenge:



<ul>
<li>The device doesn&#8217;t matter (theater, laptop, iPad, television), I&#8217;m giving up all forms of video entertainment for the month.</li>
<li>I&#8217;m making an exception for work-related video (since I do work at a cable entertainment network).</li>
</ul>



I&#8217;ll let you know how it turns out!
