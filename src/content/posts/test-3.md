Poems have always meant a lot to me. I began memorizing poems early on, thanks to a pretty terrific line-up of English teachers. I mean, all the way back in second grade, I memorized The Owl and the Pussycat. My grandmother also played a huge part in my love of poetry. Not only did she read me poems, and encourage me to write… she used to play Scrabble and Boggle with me to help strengthen my vocabulary.

I don’t know exactly what the appeal of poetry was to me, at least back then. I think maybe the structured nature of poetry made it more approachable to me as a young-un than a giant wall of text. There’s something about the “structure” of poetry, the idea that there was a format or a limitation made it easier to process, easier to know what you’re getting into. Kind of like the 15-second limit on Vine, 140 characters on Twitter, 3 minutes on Spoken... those limitations made it a little easier to consume.  And also made it easer to create instead of just facing a giant blank white page.

Anyway, whatever the reason, poetry sunk it’s metaphysical talons into me and never let go.

So I wanted to incorporate poetry into some of my pieces, so I’m starting something I’m calling Filíóchta Friday. Filíóchta is Irish for poetry. I know it’s a bit of a stretch, but my wife insisted I needed to alliterate, so… Filíóchta Friday it is. Every Friday, I’ll share a poem that’s meant something to me and maybe a little commentary on it. Typically, I’ll share poems by other people, but since this is the first, I thought it would be nice to share the first poem I ever wrote:

*In The Bleak Midwinter*

In the bleak midwinter
When the sky is dapple gray
As the horses in the barnyard
Munching on their bales of hay;

The wind is whistling through the trees,
And the strong winds blow.
The tall trees, mainly evergreens
Are tipped with pure, white snow

The children on the hillside
Who are having such a ball
Have scarcely noticed winter
Steal away the fall.

The children now are walking
In a white wonderland,
And hoping that this winter
Might never, ever end.

Plagiarized title and watery adjectives aside, this poem means a lot to me because when I read it to my grandmother, she praised me for the “stealing away the fall” line. And that encouragement is one of the main reasons I kept at poetry.
